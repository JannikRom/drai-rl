\subsection{Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (TD3)}
\label{sec:td3}

We implement Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic Policy Gradient (TD3)~\cite{fujimoto2018:TD3} for the hockey environment's continuous control tasks.
TD3 is a model-free, off-policy actor--critic algorithm that extends DDPG~\cite{lillicrap2015:DDPG} by explicitly reducing overestimation bias and stabilizing policy updates, which is crucial in continuous action domains.

TD3 follows the deterministic policy gradient framework~\cite{silver2014:DPG}.
The actor represents a deterministic policy \(a=\pi_\phi(s)\) and is trained to maximize the expected discounted return
\begin{equation}
J(\phi) = \mathbb{E}\Big[ \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t r(s_t, \pi_\phi(s_t)) \Big].
\end{equation}
A critic \(Q_\theta(s,a)\) approximates the action-value function \(Q^\pi(s,a)\) and is trained from replay buffer samples using bootstrapped Bellman targets~\cite{lillicrap2015:DDPG}.
The actor update is computed via the deterministic policy gradient
\begin{equation}
\nabla_\phi J(\phi) \approx \mathbb{E}_{s \sim \mathcal{D}}\!\left[ \nabla_a Q_{\theta}(s,a)\vert_{a=\pi_\phi(s)} \nabla_\phi \pi_\phi(s) \right].
\end{equation}
While this approach provides informative gradients in continuous control, DDPG-style methods can become unstable when the critic is inaccurate: overestimated Q-values directly induce overly optimistic policy updates and may lead to divergence~\cite{fujimoto2018:TD3}.

TD3 addresses these issues with three interacting modifications~\cite{fujimoto2018:TD3}.
First, \textbf{clipped double Q-learning} reduces overestimation by learning two independent critics \(Q_{\theta_1}\) and \(Q_{\theta_2}\) and using a conservative target based on the minimum of the two target critics,
\begin{equation}
y = r + \gamma(1-d) \min_{i=1,2} Q_{\theta_i'}(s', \tilde{a}),
\end{equation}
where \(d\) denotes episode termination and \(\theta_i'\) are target network parameters.
Each critic is optimized by minimizing the squared Bellman error over a minibatch of size \(N\),
\begin{equation}
L_{Q_i} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{j=1}^N \big( y_j - Q_{\theta_i}(s_j, a_j) \big)^2.
\end{equation}

Second, \textbf{target policy smoothing} regularizes the bootstrapping target to avoid exploiting narrow, erroneous peaks in the value estimate.
Instead of directly evaluating the target action \(\pi_{\phi'}(s')\), TD3 perturbs it with clipped Gaussian noise:
\begin{equation}
\tilde{a} = \operatorname{clip}\left( \pi_{\phi'}(s') + \epsilon,\, a_{\min},\, a_{\max} \right),\quad
\epsilon \sim \operatorname{clip}\left(\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma), -c, c\right).
\end{equation}
This encourages the critics to learn smoother Q-functions by enforcing similar values for nearby actions~\cite{fujimoto2018:TD3}.

Third, \textbf{delayed policy updates} reduce variance in the policy gradient by updating the actor less frequently than the critics (typically every \(K=2\) critic updates).
In addition, TD3 uses slowly-changing target networks for both actor and critics, updated via Polyak averaging:
\begin{equation}
\theta_i' \leftarrow \tau \theta_i + (1-\tau) \theta_i',\quad
\phi' \leftarrow \tau \phi + (1-\tau) \phi'.
\end{equation}
This yields a more stable learning objective and empirically improves training robustness in continuous control~\cite{fujimoto2018:TD3}.

Training is performed off-policy with a replay buffer \(\mathcal{D}\) storing transitions \((s_t,a_t,r_t,s_{t+1},d_t)\).
At each environment step we add exploration noise to the executed action, \(a_t = \pi_\phi(s_t) + \eta_t\), and clip the result to the action bounds before interacting with the environment.
For each gradient step, we sample minibatches from \(\mathcal{D}\), update both critics towards the target \(y\), and update the actor only every \(K\) steps using the current critic gradient.

TD3 is well-suited for the hockey task because the environment combines short-horizon precision (interceptions and shots) with long-horizon positioning and defense.
In such settings, optimistic value errors can quickly translate into unrealistic aggressive actions; TD3's conservative targets, smoothed bootstrapping, and delayed actor updates mitigate this failure mode and provide a strong baseline for continuous control~\cite{fujimoto2018:TD3}.

\subsection{Soft Actor-Critic}
\ac{SAC} is an off-policy actor-critic algorithm designed for optimizing stochastic policies in continuous action spaces. Our implementation follows the framework introduced by \cite{haarnoja2018soft}, which augments the standard maximum reward objective with an entropy maximization term \cite{ziebart2010}. This approach encourages wider exploration and improves robustness by seeking a policy that maximizes the trade-off between expected return and entropy:
\begin{equation}
J(\pi) = \sum_{t=0}^{T} \mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{s}_t, \mathbf{a}_t) \sim \rho_\pi} \left[ r(\mathbf{s}_t, \mathbf{a}_t) + \alpha \mathcal{H}(\pi(\cdot | \mathbf{s}_t)) \right]
\end{equation}
where $\rho_\pi$ denotes the state-action distribution induced by policy $\pi$ and $\mathcal{H}$ represents the entropy of the policy at state $\mathbf{s}_t$.

The \ac{SAC} algorithm consists of an actor, critic, and an entropy regularization term governed by a temperature parameter $\alpha$.
The actor is parameterized by a neural network that defines a Gaussian distribution over the continuous action space by estimating the mean $\mu_\theta(s)$ and standard deviation $\sigma_\theta(s)$ in its output layer. 

To ensure differentiability of the sampling process with respect to the policy parameters, the reparameterization trick is applied \cite{haarnoja2018soft, kingma2014auto} . Instead of sampling actions directly from the Gaussian distribution, actions are expressed as
$a = \mu_\theta(s) + \sigma_\theta(s) \odot \epsilon$ where $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$ is an standard normal random variable.

The sampled actions are passed through a $\tanh$ function and scaled to match the action bounds of the environment. To correctly account for this squashing transformation in the entropy term, the log-probability is adjusted using the determinant of the Jacobian of the $\tanh$ transformation.
The actor is trained on the maximum entropy objective \cite{haarnoja2018soft}:
\begin{equation}
J(\pi_\phi) = \mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{s}_t \sim \mathcal{D})}\left[ \mathbb{E}_{(\mathbf{a}_t) \sim \pi_\phi} \left[ \alpha \log \pi_\phi(a_t|s_t) - Q_\theta(s_t, a_t) \right]\right]
\end{equation}
where for each state sampled from the replay buffer $\mathcal{D}$, an action is sampled from the actor. The critic estimates the value for each state-action pair.

As presented in the follow-up work \cite{haarnoja2018algorithms}, we employ two Q-networks for the critic instead of a single Q-network and a value network as originally proposed. The soft value function can be implicitly derived from the soft Q-function by combining the Q-values with the policy entropy term.

During training, two independent Q-networks estimate the value of a state-action pair. To mitigate overestimation bias, soft policy evaluation is performed using the minimum of the two Q-values, which provides a more conservative value estimate \cite{Fujimoto2018Addressing}.
The critic is trained using a mean squared Bellman error loss:

\begin{equation} 
  J_Q(\theta_i) = \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal D} \left[ (Q_{\theta_i}(s_t,a_t) - y_t)^2 \right]
\end{equation}

with target value

\begin{equation}
  y_t = r_t + \gamma \left( \min_{j=1,2} Q_{\bar{\theta}_j}(s_{t+1},a_{t+1}) - \alpha \log \pi_\phi(a_{t+1}|s_{t+1}) \right)
\end{equation}

The target networks $\bar{\theta}_j$ are updated using soft updates with a factor $\tau$, which improves training stability by preventing rapid divergence between the online and target critics.

Furthermore, automatic parameter tuning is implemented, which was also introduced in the follow-up paper \cite{haarnoja2018algorithms}. The temperature parameter $\alpha$ is optimized to match a target policy entropy, allowing the agent to automatically balance exploration and exploitation. The temperature is updated by minimizing

\begin{equation}
J(\alpha) = \mathbb{E}_{a_t \sim \pi_\phi} \left[ -\alpha \left( \log \pi_\phi(a_t|s_t) + \bar{\mathcal{H}} \right) \right],
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\mathcal{H}}$ denotes the target entropy, typically set to $-\text{dim}(\mathcal{A})$ as recommended in \cite{haarnoja2018algorithms}. The parameter $\alpha$ is optimized using a gradient-based method.

\subsubsection*{Replay Buffer}
Since both implemented algorithms are off-policy, training relies on data stored in a replay buffer.  We implemented standard \ac{ER}, which improves data efficiency by reusing past interactions and reducing correlations between subsequent samples. Transitions are stored as tuples $(s_t, a_t, r_t, s_{t+1})$ in a finite buffer. During training, batches are sampled uniformly from the buffer.

However, uniform sampling treats all transitions equally, although some experiences may be more informative for learning. To address this limitation, we additionally implement \ac{PER}.

\paragraph{Prioritized Experience Replay}
\ac{PER} assigns higher sampling probabilities to transitions with larger temporal-difference (TD) errors, under the assumption that such samples provide more informative learning signals \cite{Schaul2015PrioritizedER}. Instead of uniform sampling, each transition $i$ is associated with a priority value $p_i = |\delta_i| + \varepsilon$, where where $\delta_i$ denotes the TD error and $\varepsilon > 0$ ensures non-zero probability. Transitions are sampled according to
\begin{equation}
P(i) = \frac{p_i^\alpha}{\sum_k p_k^\alpha},
\end{equation}
$\alpha$ determines the degree of prioritization, whereby $\alpha = 0$ would correspond to uniform sampling. Prioritized sampling introduces a bias, which is why importance sampling (IS) weights are used to correct the update:
\begin{equation}
w_i = \left( \frac{1}{N} \cdot \frac{1}{P(i)} \right)^\beta,
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the buffer size and $\beta$ controls the strength of bias correction \cite{Schaul2015PrioritizedER}.
