\section{Evaluation}\label{sec:eval}
In this section, we evaluate the performance of the implemented algorithms and the proposed improvements across multiple experimental settings. The evaluation is structured in three stages.
First, we assess the general performance and stability of the algorithms in three commonly used benchmark environments.
Second, we analyze training behavior in the hockey environment, with particular focus on the impact of the introduced modifications, including self-play and other algorithmic adjustments. 
Finally, we evaluate the relative playing strength of the resulting agents in a competitive tournament setting. Match outcomes are aggregated and modeled using the Placket-Luce rating system in order to obtain a grounded ranking of agents. This allows a fundamental comparison of different training configurations that goes beyond the performance metrics of a single run.
\subsection{Simple Environments}
We evaluate both \ac{SAC} and \ac{TD3} in three standard benchmark environments: Pendulum, Lunar Lander, and Half Cheetah.  For each algorithm, we compare training with standard \ac{ER} and with \ac{PER} in order to assess the effect of prioritized sampling on learning dynamics and final performance.
All algorithmic variants converge in Pendulum (200k steps) and LunarLander (800k steps), reaching comparable final returns. In these environments, \ac{PER} does not lead to substantial performance differences.

In contrast, clearer distinctions emerge in HalfCheetah (800k steps). Both SAC and TD3 benefit substantially from prioritized replay, clearly outperforming their respective \ac{ER} variants. Among all configurations, SAC with \ac{PER} achieves the highest final results in this environment, outperforming the other approaches. The results further indicate that SAC generally converges faster than TD3 across environments, as reflected in steeper initial learning curves (see Appendix \ref{app:simple_losses}).

\subsection{Hockey Environment}

After validating the algorithms on standard continuous control benchmarks, we evaluate 
them in the hockey environment. Each variant is trained for $2.5 \times 10^6$ timesteps 
against a mixture of the weak and strong basic opponent without self-play, serving as a 
first assessment of how the different improvements affect learning. Evaluation rewards are 
computed exclusively against the strong opponent using the sparse reward ($-10$ loss, 
$0$ draw, $+10$ win). Figure~\ref{fig:hockey_rewards} shows learning curves for a 
representative subset of tested configurations.

    \centering

    \caption{Avg. episode reward (mean ± eval variability).}
    \label{fig:hockey_rewards}
\end{figure}


SAC converges reliably and quickly due to its automatic entropy regularization and 
effective off-policy data reuse. Adding reward shaping accelerates early learning, 
as penalizing passivity and rewarding puck contact guide the agent to engage with the 
puck sooner. However, aggressively weighted shaping rewards may partially overshadow 
the dominant win signal, causing the agent to prioritize intermediate behaviors over 
scoring, which manifests as a slightly delayed convergence. Based on these observations, 
we adopt mild reward shaping to preserve the primacy of the win signal.

TD3 with standard white noise converges slower than SAC, as the deterministic policy 
relies entirely on externally injected noise for exploration. Replacing white noise with 
pink noise yields a clear improvement: the temporal correlation encourages the agent to 
commit to exploratory directions over consecutive timesteps, producing more purposeful 
puck-approach behavior and faster convergence, consistent with the findings of 
Eberhard et al.~\cite{eberhard2023pink}.

Contrary to expectations, TD3 with PER converges slower than TD3 Basic. In early 
training, priorities change rapidly as the value function is still far from converged, 
and the sampling distribution may become dominated by a small set of transitions. 
We assume this instability, combined with the importance-sampling correction dampening 
updates during the $\beta$ annealing phase, outweighs the benefit of focused sampling 
in this setting. All variants ultimately converge and reliably beat the strong opponent.

Additional training diagnostics including actor and critic losses as well as the SAC 
entropy temperature $\alpha$ are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:hockey_curves}. 
The entropy temperature decays steadily over training, reflecting increasing policy 
confidence. The TD3 actor losses decrease monotonically into negative territory as 
expected, and critic losses remain bounded and gradually decrease across all variants, 
indicating stable value function learning.

\medskip
Based on these experiments, both SAC and TD3 were configured with low-weight reward shaping and standard experience replay. 
For TD3, pink noise was selected as the exploration strategy given its faster convergence. 
In both cases, deeper networks with two to three hidden layers of moderate width outperformed shallower alternatives, 
suggesting the hockey environment benefits from additional representational capacity, while networks 
that were too deep or too wide introduced instability. These configurations serve as the 
baseline for subsequent self-play fine-tuning, with full hyperparameters listed in 
Tables~\ref{tab:sac_hyperparams} and~\ref{tab:td3_hyperparams} in the appendix.
