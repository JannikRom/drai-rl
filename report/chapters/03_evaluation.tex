\section{Evaluation}\label{sec:eval}

We evaluate both algorithms across two settings: standard continuous control benchmarks 
to validate the base implementations, and the hockey environment to assess the full 
pipeline including reward shaping, exploration noise, and self-play.

\subsection{Simple Environments}
We evaluate both \ac{SAC} and \ac{TD3} in three standard benchmark environments: Pendulum, Lunar Lander, and Half Cheetah.  For each algorithm, we compare training with standard \ac{ER} and with \ac{PER} in order to assess the effect of prioritized sampling on learning dynamics and final performance.
All algorithmic variants converge in Pendulum (200k steps) and LunarLander (800k steps), reaching comparable final returns. In these environments, \ac{PER} does not lead to substantial performance differences.

In contrast, clearer distinctions emerge in HalfCheetah (800k steps). Both SAC and TD3 benefit substantially from prioritized replay, clearly outperforming their respective \ac{ER} variants. Among all configurations, SAC with \ac{PER} achieves the highest final results in this environment, outperforming the other approaches. The results further indicate that SAC generally converges faster than TD3 across environments, as reflected in steeper initial learning curves (see Appendix X). The hyperparameters used for all experiments are listed in Appendix Y.

\subsection{Hockey Environment}

After validating the algorithms on standard benchmarks, we evaluate them in the hockey 
environment. Each variant is trained for $2.5 \times 10^6$ timesteps against a mixture 
of the weak and strong basic opponent without self-play. Evaluation rewards are computed 
exclusively against the strong opponent using the sparse reward ($-10$ loss, $0$ draw, 
$+10$ win). Figure~\ref{fig:hockey_rewards} shows learning curves for a representative 
subset of tested configurations.

    \label{fig:hockey_rewards}
\end{figure}

\medskip
SAC converges reliably and quickly due to its automatic entropy regularization and 
effective off-policy data reuse. Adding reward shaping accelerates early learning, as 
penalizing passivity and rewarding puck contact guide the agent to engage with the puck 
sooner. However, aggressively weighted shaping rewards may partially overshadow the 
dominant win signal, causing the agent to prioritize intermediate behaviors over scoring. 
Based on these observations, we adopt mild reward shaping to preserve the primacy of the 
win signal.

\medskip
TD3 with standard white noise converges slower than SAC, as the deterministic policy 
relies entirely on externally injected noise for exploration. Replacing white noise with 
pink noise yields a clear improvement: the temporal correlation encourages the agent to 
commit to exploratory directions over consecutive timesteps, producing more purposeful 
puck-approach behavior and faster convergence~\cite{eberhard2023pink}. Contrary to 
expectations, TD3 with PER converges slower than TD3 Basic: priorities change rapidly 
early in training, and the sampling distribution may become dominated by a small set of 
transitions, an instability we assume outweighs the benefit of focused sampling here. 
All variants ultimately converge and reliably beat the strong opponent.

Additional diagnostics including actor and critic losses and the SAC entropy temperature 
$\alpha$ are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:hockey_curves}. Based on these experiments, 
both SAC and TD3 were configured with low-weight reward shaping, standard experience 
replay, and for TD3 pink noise exploration. Deeper networks with two to three hidden 
layers of moderate width outperformed shallower alternatives, while going too deep or 
too wide introduced instability. These configurations serve as the baseline for 
self-play fine-tuning, with full hyperparameters in 
Tables~\ref{tab:sac_hyperparams} and~\ref{tab:td3_hyperparams}.

\subsubsection*{Self-Play and Tournament Evaluation}

Both SAC and TD3 were subsequently fine-tuned with the self-play framework described 
in Section~\ref{subsec:self_play}, where the growing opponent pool continuously 
challenges the agent with stronger versions of itself.

To evaluate agents throughout the development process, we implemented a round-robin 
tournament in which every participant plays $n$ episodes as both the left and right 
player against every other participant. Rankings are determined via 
TrueSkill~\cite{herbrich2006trueskill}, a Bayesian rating system updating a skill 
estimate $\mu_i$ and uncertainty $\sigma_i$ after every matchup, providing more robust 
rankings than simple win-rate averaging. Training progress was tracked via TensorBoard, 
logging standard metrics alongside self-play specific quantities such as win rate 
against the pool and pool size over time, which proved valuable for monitoring 
curriculum progression.

\medskip
Inspecting individual games of top-ranked agents revealed a recurring pattern: several 
agents had learned to exploit a specific shooting angle, deflecting the puck off the 
wall to score. While effective against many opponents, these agents lost unexpectedly 
to lower-ranked agents with a different play style, a clear sign of overfitting to a 
narrow strategy. To address this, we fine-tuned our best existing agents against a 
pre-trained opponent pool containing checkpoints of competent SAC and TD3 agents 
trained under different seeds and configurations. A slightly reduced learning rate 
preserved previously learned behavior while adapting to the more diverse opposition.

\medskip
The final tournament results are provided in Table~\ref{tab:tournament_results} in the 
appendix. Agents trained with the pre-trained opponent pool consistently ranked highest, 
confirming that stylistic diversity in the training curriculum is key to producing 
strong and generalizable agents.
